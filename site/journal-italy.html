Journal — Bari, Italy

The Thing We
Keep Running From

piano, piano
The Collection · Bari, Italy

I told you once that the world runs on two speeds. Routine, which it calls living. Complacency, which it calls comfort. And that I was chasing a third thing, something slower.

I found it in Italy.

Here, nothing is in a hurry to prove it exists. The buildings took centuries and no one minded. The afternoons stretch long and shameless. When it rains, the whole town lies down, and no one apologizes for the day being what it is.

I used to think patience was something you endured. A held breath, a gritted tooth, waiting badly for the good part to arrive. Italy taught me it's the opposite. Patience is trust. It's knowing the good part is already on its way, and refusing to ruin it by grabbing too soon. The fig ripens when it ripens. The meal takes the whole evening, because that is how long joy takes.

We rush because stillness is honest, and honesty can be frightening. When there is nothing left to do, the things we have been outrunning finally sit down beside us. But so does everything we have been too busy to feel. The warmth of the sun on old stone. The particular gold of a late afternoon. The small, holy boredom of having nowhere to be. You cannot skip ahead to the beauty. You have to sit through the quiet to earn it.

And once I stopped rushing, Italy began handing me things I hadn't known to ask for. A song drifting from a window I would never find again. An old couple walking slower than time. A whole evening that belonged to no one and nothing, least of all a clock. None of it can be chased. All of it arrives quietly, the moment you stop chasing.

Nothing beautiful was ever rushed into being. Not a fig, not a city, not a woman becoming who she's meant to be. So I have stopped pacing at the door of my own life. I am letting it bloom in its own hour.

There's more the slow taught me. Some of it stays mine.
